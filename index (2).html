Introduction — What Is Democratic Backsliding?

Understanding Democratic Backsliding

A democracy that begins to lose pieces of itself—slowly, almost quietly—while still appearing to function as usual.

When we talk about democratic backsliding, we're describing a process that's often hard to detect in its early stages. It helps to think of democracy as a living system whose health can deteriorate long before it collapses outright.

The First Characteristic: Gradual Decline

Democratic backsliding rarely announces itself with fanfare. Unlike a dramatic coup or authoritarian takeover—moments when institutions fall like a building suddenly losing its scaffolding—democratic erosion happens quietly.

Instead of sudden collapse, backsliding arrives in small, seemingly insignificant steps:

Legal Procedures

New laws thatseems reasonable but gradually restrict rights

Institutional "Repairs"

Changes presented as improvements that weaken checks and balances

Executive Power Expansion

Incremental stretching of presidential or prime ministerial authority

Concrete Examples

  • Judicial Abuse: A government using courts to imprison journalists, disguised as routine legal proceedings
  • Electoral Manipulation: Quietly replacing election administrators with political loyalists while maintaining electoral formalities
  • Term Limit Erosion: Weakening presidential term limits through court rulings that reinterpret constitutional meaning, avoiding formal amendments

No dramatic announcement is made, yet something essential shifts. This is why we must be attentive to the accumulation of these subtle changes: individually they seem manageable, but together they erode the system from within.

The Second Characteristic: Unfolding Mechanisms

Backsliding also has distinctive mechanisms through which it operates. These are the specific ways in which democratic quality deteriorates:

Erosion Without Abolition

Democracies may not abolish elections, but they erode public trust in them

Norm Weakening

They may not end universal suffrage, but they weaken the norms that protect it

Constraints on executive power—whether written into law or upheld by long-established political expectations—may begin to loosen, one strand at a time (Little and Meng 155, 159).

The democratic system continues to exist, but its internal stability is slowly unravelling.

To truly understand this regression, we must ask ourselves: What exactly is slipping away? Before we can diagnose the decline, we must return to democracy itself—what it promises, what sustains it, and what must remain intact for it to thrive.

What is Democracy?

To understand democratic backsliding, we first need to grasp what constitutes a healthy democracy. Let's explore the foundational elements that make democracy work.

The Core of Democracy

Democracy is an integral concept in contemporary society (Munck 22). At its core, it is often understood as "rule by the demos," meaning that political authority ultimately belongs to the majority rather than to a privileged few.

Two Fundamental Principles

To make this possible, democracy depends on two fundamental principles:

Political Freedom

Allows individuals to express preferences without coercion

Political Equality

Ensures that each citizen's rights and influence are treated equally in the political process (Munck 11)

These principles matter because they determine whether democratic rule genuinely reflects the people's will and whether leaders remain accountable. Political freedom gives citizens space to voice their choices without fear, while political equality prevents those choices from being overshadowed by disparities in status or resources (Schmitter and Karl 6-7, 9).

"Together, they allow democracy to function as a system of ongoing expression, participation, and competition."

Beyond Values: Institutions Matter

Yet values alone do not sustain democracy. Freedom and equality explain why democracy matters, but they do not explain how it operates in practice.

For that, we must view democracy as a set of institutions and procedures that translate these principles into action (Schmitter and Karl 4).

Key Democratic Institutions

  • Free and fair elections
  • Competitive political parties
  • Independent judiciary
  • Free press and media
  • Active civil society organizations

These form the institutional framework that enables democratic commitments to produce real political outcomes (Schmitter and Karl 6-7; Gorokhovskaia and Grothe 15, 35).

Without transparent electoral rules, checks on executive authority, and protections for dissent, freedom and equality remain largely symbolic.

Three Interdependent Foundations

From this institutional perspective, democracy rests on three crucial pillars:

1. Freedom of Expression

The freedom of citizens to express their opinions and the independence of the media (Gorokhovskaia and Grothe 16)

2. Political Participation

Ensures equal standing for all citizens in the political process (Gorokhovskaia and Grothe 6)

3. Political Competition

Ensures that public power remains substitutable rather than entrenched (Schmitter and Karl 4-5)

Democracy possesses representativeness, openness, and accountability only when these foundations operate together.

Understanding Different Levels of Democracy

Minimalist Democracy

Requires only regular and competitive elections through which governments can be replaced

(Przeworski and Limongi 178)

Maximalist Democracy

Incorporates additional elements such as political freedoms, the rule of law, media independence, civil society participation, and institutional checks and balances

(Schmitter and Karl 7-9, 11)

This broader framework highlights that democracy is not simply electoral competition but a political order in which freedom, equality, and participation are institutionalized.

The Path to Democratic Backsliding

Taken together, these institutionalized elements sustain democratic stability. When they weaken—through reduced political competition, unequal participation, or shrinking civil liberties—the quality of democracy deteriorates. Over time, such deterioration can lead to what we call democratic backsliding.

Mechanisms of Democratic Backsliding

Mechanisms of Democratic Backsliding

How democracy erodes from within through four distinct pathways

The Essence of Modern Backsliding

"Democratic backsliding often uses 'legal tools' for 'illegal purposes' — a combination of legalism + incrementalism + operation within institutions."

1

Executive Aggrandizement

The expansion of executive power is often accompanied by the redistribution of resources and personnel by the rulers. This redistribution is usually achieved through bribery, appeasement, and more covert selective persecution, thereby consolidating their political control (Levitsky and Way 53).

Four Key Areas of Consolidation

Elections

Despite regular elections, the process can be rife with abuses of state power, biased media reporting, and (often violent) harassment of opposition candidates and activists (Levitsky and Way 55).

Example: In Peru, Fujimori won re-election in 2000 but was forced to resign months later due to electoral fraud scandals (Levitsky and Way 55).

Legislature

Those in power may attempt to circumvent or even shut down the legislature when it opposes executive interests (Levitsky and Way 56).

Judiciary

The expansion often attempts to subjugate the judiciary to executive power.

Example: In Russia, Yeltsin cut off the court's telephone lines and removed guards after the Constitutional Court ruled the decree dissolving parliament unconstitutional (Levitsky and Way 56).

Media

The executive branch actively seeks to suppress independent media, but uses more subtle repressive mechanisms than in a completely authoritarian regime (Levitsky and Way 58).

2

Strategic Harassment and Repression

This mechanism is characterized by subtlety, legitimacy, and systematic attacks on key competitive areas of the democratic system.

Targeted Areas of Attack

Electoral Arena

Ensures the opposition is at a disadvantage through systematic manipulation (Levitsky and Way 55).

Example: The ruling party abuses state resources to deny the opposition sufficient media coverage (Levitsky and Way 55).

Judicial Arena

Attempts to subjugate the judiciary to executive power in order to eliminate legal checks and balances.

Methods include impeachment or more subtle means such as bribery, blackmail, and appeasement (Levitsky and Way 56).

Legislative Sphere

Ensures that even if the opposition uses the legislature as a venue for rallies and organizations, such activities have little substantial impact (unless there is extensive coverage by independent media) (Levitsky and Way 56).

Media Sphere

Usually manifested in the widespread use of restrictive press laws to "legally" harass journalists.

Example: In Ghana, the government used colonial-era libel laws to imprison several newspaper editors and columnists (Levitsky and Way 58).

3

Electoral Manipulation

This refers to weakening the substantive competitiveness of elections through a series of implicit and institutionalized methods, while maintaining the appearance of democratic processes.

Key Methods of Electoral Manipulation

  • Abuse of state resources and mobilization of the bureaucratic system
  • Monopolization of media exposure
  • Selective harassment of opposition using tax authorities, regulatory agencies, and the judicial system
  • Interference with voting processes by adjusting election rules
  • Manipulation of election commissions
The "Legal" Facade

The ruling party often makes it difficult for the opposition to carry out normal political activities in a seemingly "legal" environment (Levitsky and Way 53).

Although elections still exist and regime changes occasionally occur, the balance of power is often systematically tilted towards the incumbent. Because these procedures take the form of "legitimate procedural reforms," they are difficult for the international community to immediately identify and are not easily captured by common democratic indicators (Levitsky and Way 55).

4

Media Capture and Shrinking Civic Space

The ruling party systematically eliminates key mechanisms for checks and balances and suppresses critical voices while maintaining the appearance of democracy.

Media Capture

The ruling party aims to suppress independent media through subtle and covert means (Levitsky and Way 58).

Common Methods:
  • Bribery of media owners and journalists
  • Selective allocation of state advertising
  • Manipulation of media organizations' debt and taxes
Example: In Russia, the government used the debt owed by an independent television station to Gazprom to orchestrate a takeover by pro-government forces (Levitsky and Way 58).

Shrinking Civic Space

This often manifests as the ruling party using the state apparatus to covertly "strategically harass and repress" critics.

Common Methods:
  • Using tax authorities to audit critics
  • Employing subservient judicial bodies to file lawsuits
  • Using other state institutions to blackmail or "legally" harass opponents
As a result, journalists, opposition politicians, and critics may be subject to surveillance, threats, harassment, or arrest (Levitsky and Way 53).

Recognizing the Patterns

These four mechanisms rarely operate in isolation. They often work together, reinforcing each other to erode democratic institutions from within. By understanding these patterns, we can better identify early warning signs of democratic backsliding and work to counteract them.

Institutional Vulnerabilities

Why Do Some Democracies Backslide Faster?

Not all democracies erode at the same pace. Understanding the vulnerabilities that accelerate democratic backsliding is crucial for early detection and prevention.

1. Weak Institutional Strength

The Accountability Gap

Democracies with weak institutional strength suffer from deficiencies in horizontal accountability - the ability of government institutions to check and balance each other.

Judicial bypass: Leaders can more easily circumvent courts when judicial independence is weak

Legislative weakening: Executive branches can undermine parliamentary oversight

Independent bodies: Agencies like election commissions lose autonomy

"These institutions are powerless to prevent increasingly authoritarian behavior by government members." (Waldner and Lust 99)

2. Social Polarization

The "Enemy" Mentality

Political polarization creates an environment where opponents are no longer seen as competitors but as existential threats.

Threat perception: Opposing political camp portrayed as dangerous "others"

Extreme policy support: Public becomes willing to back radical measures to neutralize perceived threats

"Lesser of two evils" mentality: Voters ignore democratic threats to prevent opposition victory

"This shift in public attitudes creates space for political elites to pursue lasting dominance, bending rules and ignoring democratic norms." (Waldner and Lust 109)

The Vicious Cycle of Backsliding

Weak Institutions

Create power vacuums that leaders exploit

Power Concentration

Leaders consolidate authority

Polarization Increases

Public becomes more divided

Cycle repeats, accelerating backsliding

Key Takeaway

The speed of democratic backsliding depends largely on two interconnected factors:

  • The strength of institutional checks and balances
  • The level of social and political polarization

By addressing these vulnerabilities - strengthening institutions and reducing polarization - democracies can better resist erosion and maintain their integrity.

Global Case Studies — How Democracies Backslide in Reality

Turkey — Recep Tayyip Erdoğan

From a promising electoral democracy to competitive authoritarianism under AKP rule

1

Background — Why is Turkey a Typical Case Study?

Around 2000, Turkey appeared to be an "electoral democracy": people could vote, and there was competition between political parties, but the military still played a significant role in politics, and the party system was unstable.

The Neo-Patrimonial Transformation

With Erdoğan and the AKP coming to power in 2002, Turkey's political structure gradually changed. State institutions increasingly ceased to be independent and became extensions of the ruling party and its supporters, a model known as neo-patrimonialism (Ugur-Cinar 447-448).

Key Features of Neo-Patrimonialism

  • State resources are seen as tools for the leader to "distribute"
  • Loyalty to the ruler is more important than professionalism in the bureaucracy
  • The state is no longer entirely a "public institution," but is partially "privatized"

All of this created the structural foundation for subsequent democratic backsliding.

The EU Factor

Turkey initially hoped to join the EU, and the EU acted as an external force promoting reforms in the early 2000s-2010s. However, later, due to the refugee crisis, the EU ceased to pressure Turkey on its democratic situation and even became financially dependent on it (Ugur-Cinar 449-450). This weakened external checks and balances.

2

Institutional Vulnerabilities — Why are Institutions Themselves Easily Eroded?

Privatization of State Institutions

The administrative system becomes an extension of the ruling party.

Due to the neo-patrimonial structure, state institutions (courts, regulatory agencies, media oversight bodies, central bank) no longer maintain professional independence. This means that the neutrality of the institutions is undermined, and even if checks and balances are written into the constitution, they cannot function (Ugur-Cinar 448).

Ultimately, these institutions often become "government tools."

High Concentration of Power After the Presidential System Reform

In 2018, Turkey officially transitioned to a presidential system, resulting in two significant changes:

  • The president directly controls the economic and administrative systems and can arbitrarily replace the central bank governor and ministers (Ugur-Cinar 445)
  • The position of prime minister was abolished, eliminating the original system of checks and balances (445)

This structure not only concentrates power but also weakens accountability—making it difficult to hold the government accountable for erroneous decisions.

Politicization of the Judiciary: Undermining Judicial Independence Through "Court Packing"

The 2010 judicial referendum allowed the government to add more pro-government judges to the court system, which is what political science calls "court packing" (Ugur-Cinar 448).

The ultimate result is that judicial review loses its independence, and the courts become a tool for the ruling party to suppress the opposition.

The Ambiguity of a Hybrid Regime: Why is Turkey Difficult for the Outside World to Assess?

Scholars describe Turkey as a form of competitive authoritarianism (Ugur-Cinar 447). This means that elections still formally exist, and the opposition can still exist.

However, the competitive environment is severely unfair, with the ruling party using state resources to suppress opponents.

The ambiguity of this system leads to:

It is difficult for the internal population to realize that democracy is regressing, and at the same time, it is difficult for the outside world to exert effective pressure (447).

3

Mechanisms of Backsliding — How Does the Regime Gradually Undermine Competitiveness?

Systematic Abuse of State Resources

The government uses the state budget, public media (such as TRT), government advertising, and public works contracts to support the ruling party, creating a huge asymmetry in elections (446).

Judicial Persecution and Political Intimidation

The judicial system becomes a weapon to attack the opposition:

  • Criminal charges are brought against important opposition leaders (such as Imamoglu and Demirtas) (Ugur-Cinar 448)
  • Judges who refuse to cooperate are dismissed or transferred (448)

This selective enforcement severely undermines political competition.

Media Integration and Propaganda

Due to media reliance on government advertising budgets, a large number of media outlets have been acquired by companies closely linked to the government (Ugur-Cinar 448).

Result:

The media almost entirely speaks for the government, making it difficult for the opposition to disseminate information. Even during the 2023 elections, Erdoğan received 32 hours of coverage, while the opposition candidate only received 32 minutes (448).

Disinformation and Manipulation of Public Opinion

The ruling party uses its media advantage to spread false or fabricated information, such as using edited videos to suggest that the opposition is collaborating with terrorist organizations. This instills fear and distrust of the opposition among the public (Ugur-Cinar 449).

4

Reasons for Faster Sliding — Why is the Regression So Rapid?

Media Dependence on the Government

The government can use advertising, commercial contracts, and regulatory pressure to quickly bring the media entirely under the control of the ruling party (Ugur-Cinar 448).

The EU No Longer Exerts Pressure

Turkey has received billions of euros from the EU using the refugee issue, therefore:

  • The EU remains silent on the erosion of democracy in Turkey
  • External checks and balances are completely weakened
  • The rulers gain international legitimacy (Ugur-Cinar 450)

Maintaining the "Illusion of Competition"

Although the competition is unfair, the form of elections is still maintained, leading the opposition and international observers to mistakenly believe that Turkey is still a "democratic country" (Ugur-Cinar 451).

The "Narrative of Fear" is More Effective

Under strong media control, the government constantly emphasizes:

  • External threats
  • Terrorism
  • National security

Leading voters to choose a "safe leader," accelerating democratic backsliding (Ugur-Cinar 449).

Outcome

Turkey has clearly entered into competitive authoritarianism. The regression was driven by supporters and proceeded rapidly, but it faces greater opposition compared to Hungary.

India — Narendra Modi

From the world's largest democracy to an electoral autocracy in just a decade

1

Background

India is widely regarded as the world's largest and most enduring post-colonial democracy (Vaishnav 35).

The Democratic Decline

Since 2014, the rise of the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) under Narendra Modi has coincided with a notable democratic decline.

Evidence in Indices

This trend is reflected in sharp drops across Freedom House, V-Dem, and European University Institute (EUI) indices (35).

"India's case challenges the assumption that long-lasting democracies automatically develop self-protective institutions: even deep-rooted democracies can erode rapidly when oversight institutions weaken."

2

Institutional Vulnerability

Parliamentary System + Strong Majority → Centralized Executive Power

A single-party majority (as in BJP's victories in 2014 and 2019) allows the prime minister to dominate cabinet and parliament, weakening intra-elite checks (Vaishnav 37).

This enables the fast passage of executive priorities, including identity-based legislation, with limited scrutiny.

Weakening of Arbitration Bodies

Supreme Court
  • Increasingly deferential—delayed hearings on Section 370
  • Failed to halt mass detentions
  • Declined to block the election bonds scheme before elections (Vaishnav 40)
Election Commission of India (ECI)
  • Showed bias or inaction—postponed Gujarat elections
  • Selectively disciplined opposition while shielding Modi and Shah (Vaishnav 42)

Structural Dependence of Civil Society and Media

Media Vulnerability

Media dependence on government advertising allows "informal pressure" without explicit censorship (Vaishnav 48).

Civil Society Constraints

Civil society organizations face selective restrictions through the Foreign Contribution Regulation Act (FCRA), constraining funding sources (48).

3

Regression Mechanisms

Identity-Based Legal Changes (CAA/NRC)

CAA: Excludes Muslim immigrants from fast-tracked citizenship.

NRC: Risks rendering Muslim minorities stateless.

Citizenship becomes increasingly tied to religious identity rather than constitutional equality.

Informal Media Control

Tax raids, licensing threats, and ad allocation produce widespread self-censorship despite the absence of formal censorship laws (48).

Judicial and Institutional Co-optation

  • Sensitive cases are postponed, enabling government fait accompli results (Vaishnav 41)
  • Appointment panels face obstruction (39–40)
  • Investigations targeting dissident officials signal retaliation (42)

Selective Enforcement by State Agencies

Enforcement Directorate cases against opposition leaders quadrupled after 2014; 95% targeted opposition figures (45).

Horizontal accountability—central to liberal democracy—is undermined.

4

Why India's Backsliding Has Been Particularly Fast

Majority Rule Accelerates Institutional Deterioration

One-party dominance removed the coalition-era constraints (1989–2014) that once protected judicial autonomy.

Nationalist Ideology Enhances Public Acceptance

Hindu nationalism frames government actions as protective of national identity and labels dissent as "anti-national."

Structural Dependence of Media & Civil Society

Media reliant on state revenue and NGOs reliant on foreign funding are highly vulnerable to selective pressure.

Weak, Fragmented Opposition

The Congress Party's organizational decline prevents effective resistance (Vaishnav 49).

Arbitration Bodies Lacking Intrinsic Safeguards

Courts, commissions, and investigative agencies operate largely on convention rather than entrenched constitutional firewalls.

Together, these conditions create a high-speed pathway to democratic erosion: strong executive power meets weak institutional resistance, intensified by identity-driven ideology and dependent civic ecosystems.

5

Outcomes

India is now rated as "partly free" or an "electoral autocracy" across major democracy indices.

Key Areas of Democratic Decline

Civil Liberties

Judicial Independence

Media Freedom

Minority Protections

Horizontal Accountability

Hungary — Viktor Orbán

The most classic case of democratic backsliding in post-communist Europe

1

Background

Hungary transitioned to a democratic state after the collapse of communism in 1990, but its democratic foundations have remained unstable.

The "Mixed Transition" Legacy

During the transition, Hungary experienced a "mixed transition":

  • Partly relying on negotiation (such as roundtable discussions)
  • Partly due to the disunity of the opposition, leading to conflict and the breakdown of negotiations

This transition without complete consensus left room for future political struggles (Bernhard 590).

The Economic Crisis Catalyst

The economic crisis in the late 2000s severely impacted the ruling party, paving the way for the rise of Fidesz and Viktor Orbán in 2010 (Bernhard 598-599). Orbán gained significant popular support by presenting himself as the "savior of the nation," laying the foundation for his subsequent strong governance.

2

Institutional Vulnerabilities

Incomplete Transition Process

Hungary's democratic transition itself was unstable—it included both negotiation and conflict, thus leaving many gray areas (Bernhard 588).

Because the transition process appeared "unclean" and "incomplete," certain political forces could claim that: "The democratic transition was not truly driven by the people," but rather a "behind-the-scenes deal" between communist reformers and opposition elites (Bernhard 593).

The "Memory Warrior" Strategy

This narrative is called the "memory warrior" strategy (593). It is not about reviewing history, but about using doubts about the past to achieve current political goals:

  • Questioning the legitimacy of the democratic transition
  • Claiming that the country was betrayed by "elites who do not represent the people"
  • Providing justification for weakening existing democratic institutions (Bernhard 607)

Fidesz (Young Democrats' Alliance) is the party that most successfully utilized this narrative (Bernhard 594). By portraying the democratic transition as a "failed project manipulated by corrupt elites," Fidesz successfully constructed its political identity as the "true representative of the people," laying the foundation for subsequent institutional restructuring (Bernhard 593, 599).

The Electoral System Amplification Effect

Hungary's early electoral system was more geared towards "governability" than "representativeness."

As a result, the votes of large parties were significantly amplified, while those of small parties were severely underestimated. A slightly higher majority was enough to secure a constitutional majority (2/3) (Bernhard 599).

In 2010, Fidesz won slightly over 50% of the vote in the first round but secured 2/3 of the parliamentary seats, allowing them to unilaterally amend the constitution. This meant that from the first day Fidesz came to power, there were virtually no institutional checks and balances (Bernhard 605).

3

Mechanism of Backsliding

How did Fidesz systematically erode democracy step by step? Hungary's democratic backsliding did not happen suddenly, but through a series of "gradual and institutionalized changes."

Weakening Horizontal Accountability

Horizontal accountability refers to the mutual oversight of the government by internal state institutions, such as courts, election commissions, and auditing bodies. Fidesz systematically weakened these institutions (Bernhard 600).

  • Changing electoral system rules
  • Redrawing electoral districts (gerrymandering)
  • Adjusting the proportional representation system to benefit the ruling party (Bernhard 599)

Weakening Social Accountability

Social accountability comes from civil society, the media, universities, and research institutions. Fidesz also gradually eroded these forces:

  • Labeling foreign-funded organizations as "foreign agents," thereby creating social stigmatization and regulatory pressure (Bernhard 599)
  • Controlling universities and research institutions
    • The Central European University (CEU) was forced to leave Hungary
    • The Hungarian Academy of Sciences lost its independent administrative power (Bernhard 600)

Promoting the Ideology of "Illiberal Democracy"

In 2014, Orbán publicly stated:

  • Liberal democracy is outdated, and Hungary should develop an "illiberal democracy" (Bernhard 599)
  • He cited China, Russia, and Turkey as models for development
  • He described civil society as "agents of foreign powers" (Bernhard 599-600)

This is a form of "legitimization work" at the cultural level, providing a value foundation for institutional backsliding.

4

Reasons for Faster Sliding

Hungary regressed faster than Poland or Turkey due to the combination of three structural factors:

The "Supervision Paradox" of EU Membership

Joining the EU should theoretically promote democracy, but Hungary experienced the opposite effect:

Candidate countries are subject to strict supervision, but once they become full members, the enforcement mechanisms are limited (Bernhard 596, 606).

Therefore, Fidesz was able to erode democracy while continuing to receive EU funds, and ultimately promote an "anti-Brussels" nationalist narrative (Bernhard 606-607). As a result, external constraints weakened, accelerating internal backsliding.

The "Perfect Timing" of Two Key External Crises

The Global Financial Crisis (2007–2009)
  • The ruling party MSzP's support collapsed
  • Fidesz easily won a constitutional majority in 2010
  • The crisis fueled Fidesz's initial acceleration of backsliding (Bernhard 598, 606)
The Refugee Crisis (2015)

When approval ratings declined and the far-right Jobbik party rose:

  • Orbán exploited xenophobic sentiments to create a "national threat" narrative
  • He emphasized resistance to refugees and EU demands (Bernhard 600)
  • He successfully portrayed himself as "the only force protecting Hungarian culture"

Institutional Design + Weak Social Constraints = "High-Speed Race Car Effect"

  • The electoral system makes a constitutional majority easy to obtain
  • The weak independence of the courts and media makes them easily captured (Bernhard 605)

Ultimately, this resulted in: Fidesz only needed to launch one crisis narrative to quickly consolidate power, with almost no institutional or social counter-forces.

Outcome

Hungary has shifted from democracy to competitive authoritarianism.

Academic Debate: Gradual Erosion vs. Rapid Autocratization

What's the nature of democratic backsliding? Our case studies reveal competing perspectives on this critical question.

The Central Question

"Is democratic backsliding a 'gradual erosion' or 'rapid autocratization'?"

Our analysis of three global cases—Hungary, India, and Turkey—reveals complex patterns that challenge simplistic categorizations of democratic decline.

A

Viewpoint A: Gradual Erosion

Proponents of this perspective argue that democratic backsliding typically occurs through incremental steps:

Constitutional Manipulation

Governments gradually amend constitutions to expand executive power while maintaining democratic appearances.

Electoral Engineering

Strategic redrawing of districts and changes to electoral rules systematically advantage the ruling party.

Judicial Capture

Courts are gradually packed with loyalists, undermining judicial independence over time.

Media Control

Media outlets are slowly co-opted through regulatory pressure, ownership changes, and financial leverage.

This perspective emphasizes "executive aggrandizement" as the primary mechanism—slowly concentrating power while maintaining democratic facades.

B

Viewpoint B: Rapid Autocratization

This perspective highlights that certain critical junctures can accelerate democratic decline dramatically:

The Turkish Example

Following the 2016 failed coup, President Erdoğan rapidly implemented:

  • State of emergency declarations
  • Mass purges of the military, judiciary, and bureaucracy
  • Accelerated centralization of executive power
  • Intensified media censorship and opposition suppression

While acknowledging gradual erosion may precede these moments, this perspective emphasizes how crises can trigger sudden, dramatic shifts toward autocracy.

Comparative Case Analysis

Hungary: The Gradual Erosion Model

Orbán's Fidesz party implemented a systematic, step-by-step approach to democratic backsliding:

  • Constitutional amendments (2011)
  • Judicial restructuring and court packing
  • Media regulatory changes and consolidation
  • Electoral system modifications favoring the ruling party

"Hungary demonstrates how democracy can wither slowly through institutional means, without overt authoritarian displays."

India: Systemic Vulnerability Revealed

India's democratic decline exposes deeper structural weaknesses:

  • Parliamentary system with strong majority enabling executive dominance
  • Long-term erosion of institutional independence
  • Identity-based polarization accelerating under Modi
  • Reliance on political fragmentation rather than institutional safeguards

"India's case shows that even long-established democracies can erode when institutional design relies on political competition rather than formal checks and balances."

Turkey: Acceleration Point Model

Turkey represents a hybrid pattern with distinct phases:

  • Pre-2016: Gradual neo-patrimonial transformation under AKP
  • 2016: Failed coup as acceleration point for rapid autocratization
  • Post-2016: Systematic purge and institutional capture
  • 2018: Presidential system referendum consolidating executive power

"Turkey demonstrates how initial gradual erosion can create conditions for rapid autocratization when a triggering event occurs."

Synthesis: A Complex Reality

Our comparative analysis suggests that democratic backsliding is neither purely gradual nor purely rapid, but often involves:

  1. Foundational vulnerabilities that create structural openings for democratic erosion
  2. Initial gradual erosion of democratic norms, institutions, and practices
  3. Potential acceleration points (crises, coups, electoral victories) that can dramatically speed up the process
  4. Path-dependent outcomes shaped by historical legacies, institutional design, and international context

Understanding these complex dynamics is crucial for developing effective strategies to prevent and reverse democratic backsliding worldwide.
